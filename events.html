Intrams

1. What is the most important thing I learned from the event?
One important thing I learned during the Intramurals was how teamwork really affects the outcome of a game. In both basketball and volleyball, you can’t just rely on yourself. You have to listen to your teammates, support each other, and trust everyone to do their part.

2. How can I apply what I learned in real-life situations?
The things I learned can help me in group projects or any team activities, even outside of sports. For example, when working with classmates, I now understand that communication and cooperation are key just like in a game. It’s not about being the best on your own, but about helping the whole group succeed.

3. Did I actively participate in the event? How?
Yes, I did. I played both basketball and volleyball for our team. I showed up to practice, joined the games, and gave my best effort on the court. I also encouraged my teammates and stayed focused during each match.

4. If I were to teach this topic/subject to a classmate, how would I explain it?
I’d explain that Intramurals is more than just playing sports. It’s about learning how to be a team player, how to manage pressure, and how to keep going even when things get tough. I’d also tell them that it’s a good way to build confidence and meet new people.

5. Why is it important to have an event (per subject)? Explain your answer.
Events like this help us apply what we learn in class in a fun and practical way. For PE, it makes the lessons more meaningful because we get to experience them, not just read or talk about them. It also helps build school spirit and gives students a chance to discover skills they might not realize they had.

Science Month

1. What is the most important thing I learned from the event?
Participating in Science Month taught me many valuable lessons, but the most important one was understanding the power of collaboration and how small efforts can lead to big, meaningful results. Working on the hygiene-themed project for Win’s Corner made me realize that science isn't only about formulas or experiments—it’s also about making a positive impact in our daily lives. By simply focusing on cleanliness and hygiene in our classroom, we were able to inspire others, promote healthier habits, and ultimately win the Cleanest Classroom Award. This experience showed me that science can be practical, creative, and socially meaningful, especially when it encourages behavior that leads to a better environment for everyone.

2. How can I apply what I learned in real-life situations?
One of the biggest takeaways from this project is that the principles of hygiene and cleanliness apply everywhere—not just during school events. At home, I can make sure to keep my space organized and clean, and I can also encourage my family to recycle and reuse materials whenever possible, just like we did when making the DIY toothbrush holder. In public spaces, I can be more conscious of my waste and how I contribute to the cleanliness of shared environments. Moreover, I learned that being proactive and taking initiative—whether it’s in designing something creative or helping with group tasks—can make a real difference. These are skills and habits I can apply not only in school but also in everyday life, work environments, and community activities in the future.

3. Did I actively participate in the event? How?
Yes, I did. I helped with creating our classroom’s hygiene project for Win’s Corner. I worked with my classmates to design and decorate our display, which promoted hygiene and cleanliness. I participated in brainstorming ideas, crafting materials, and ensuring that everything looked neat and creative. It was a fun activity while promoting an important message. I also helped clean, organize, and maintain the space throughout the event to ensure that our classroom stayed neat and presentable. Our collective efforts paid off when we were awarded the Cleanest Classroom, which made the experience even more rewarding.

4. If I were to teach this topic/subject to a classmate, how would I explain it?
If I had the opportunity to teach this topic to a classmate, I would begin by explaining that hygiene is not just a personal habit, but a responsibility that affects everyone in a shared space. I would talk about how our class used science and creativity to address hygiene through an interactive classroom design. I would walk them through how we reused materials to create a toothbrush holder, which not only supported hygiene but also taught us about sustainability and reducing waste. I’d emphasize that learning science isn’t limited to reading textbooks—it can involve hands-on projects that teach real-world skills like problem-solving, teamwork, and environmental awareness. I would also encourage them to think about how they could apply science in their own way to improve their surroundings.

5. Why is it important to have an event (per subject)? Explain your answer.
Having events like Science Month is important because they make learning more meaningful and engaging. While classroom lessons provide the foundation, events give students the chance to apply what they’ve learned in fun, interactive, and memorable ways. For science in particular, events like these help us see how scientific concepts connect to real-life issues—like health, hygiene, and sustainability. They also promote teamwork, creativity, and leadership skills that students might not get to develop in regular class sessions. Additionally, events foster a sense of community and friendly competition, which motivates everyone to do their best. When students are given the opportunity to take part in a subject-based event, they don’t just memorize facts—they experience the subject in action, which can have a lasting impact on how they view and value education.

I’d also encourage them to get involved in school events because it helps you grow as a person, and it gives you the chance to give back to the people who help you every day.

5. Why is it important to have an event (per subject)? Explain your answer.
Having events for each subject—or for important occasions like Teacher’s Day—helps us connect more deeply with what we’re learning and with the people around us. These events allow us to be creative, step outside of our usual routines, and apply what we’ve learned in class in more meaningful ways. They also build school spirit and a sense of community. Specifically for Teacher’s Day, it gave us the opportunity to say thank you and to show our appreciation through something fun and heartfelt. Events like this remind us that learning isn’t just about textbooks—it’s also about relationships, values, and personal growth.

A.P. Month

1. What is the most important thing I learned from the event?
One of the most important things I learned during AP Month was how valuable it is to understand different cultures and global perspectives. Representing New Zealand in the United Nations simulation taught me a lot about international relations, diplomacy, and how every country—no matter how big or small—plays a role in solving world issues. It was an eye-opening experience because it made me realize that being informed about global problems, such as climate change, peace, and equality, is essential not just for leaders but for ordinary citizens as well. It also reminded me that history and social studies aren’t just about memorizing facts—they help us understand how society works and how we can make it better.

2. How can I apply what I learned in real-life situations?
I can apply what I learned in real-life situations by being more aware of current events and how they affect not only our country but also the rest of the world. I can also practice the skills I developed during AP Month, such as critical thinking, public speaking, and teamwork. For example, during the United Nations simulation, I learned how to express my opinions respectfully while considering the perspectives of others. That’s something I can use in discussions, debates, and even in everyday life when solving problems or making decisions. It also made me more motivated to read the news, learn about global issues, and think about how we as students can contribute to positive change.

3. Did I actively participate in the event? How?
Yes, I actively participated in several parts of the event. I joined the Quiz Bee, where I reviewed and studied a lot of lessons about history and current events. I also contributed to our editorial cartoon entry, where I helped brainstorm ideas and give feedback on the final design. During the UN simulation, I represented New Zealand and delivered a speech about climate action, which helped me practice speaking in front of a crowd with confidence. All of these experiences helped me improve my academic knowledge while also developing my communication and leadership skills.

4. If I were to teach this topic/subject to a classmate, how would I explain it?
I would explain that Araling Panlipunan is not just about studying history—it’s about understanding people, cultures, and how societies develop over time. I’d tell them that AP Month is a way to make learning about social issues more interactive and exciting. For example, by joining activities like the UN simulation, Quiz Bee, or poster-making, we get to apply what we’ve learned in class to real-world topics. I’d encourage my classmate to join because it helps improve research, teamwork, and communication skills while also deepening one’s appreciation for global awareness and citizenship.

5. Why is it important to have an event (per subject)? Explain your answer.
Having events like AP Month is important because they make learning more alive and engaging. It allows students to go beyond the classroom and experience what they’re studying in a hands-on way. For Araling Panlipunan, it helps us connect history, politics, and culture with present-day issues, making the lessons more meaningful. These events also help students discover their strengths—whether in leadership, creativity, or analytical thinking—and encourage teamwork and healthy competition. Most of all, it teaches us to become more socially aware and responsible citizens who understand not just our own country but also our role in the larger global community.

Values Month

1. What is the most important thing I learned from the event?
The most important thing I learned during Values Month is that leadership is about character, humility, and responsibility, not just about holding a title. When I passed the crown as Mr. MVP, I felt proud but also emotional because it marked the end of my journey as a title holder and reminded me that true leadership means inspiring others and gracefully allowing someone else to take your place. It taught me maturity, respect, and gratitude for the opportunity I was given. At the same time, participating in the V-pop dance performance showed me the importance of teamwork, discipline, and unity, because no performance becomes successful without cooperation and support from every member. Overall, Values Month helped me realize that values are not just lessons we talk about in class, but qualities we must live out every day.

2. How can I apply what I learned in real-life situations?
I can apply what I learned by practicing humility, responsibility, and teamwork in my daily life. Passing the crown taught me how to handle success with grace and appreciation, reminding me to stay humble even when I achieve something. Through V-pop dance, I developed discipline, time management, and cooperation because we had to practice regularly, coordinate with each other, and make sure we performed as one group. These experiences can help me in group projects, school activities, leadership roles, and even in handling challenges in the future. The confidence I gained from performing and being part of the ceremony will also help me express myself better and take on new opportunities without fear.

3. Did I actively participate in the event? How?
Yes, I actively participated in Values Month by taking part in two important activities. First, I was involved in the crown passing ceremony as Mr. MVP, which was a meaningful role that symbolized leadership and responsibility. It required confidence and composure because I represented not just myself but the values connected to the title. Second, I joined the V-pop dance performance, where our group practiced hard, corrected our mistakes, and supported each other to deliver a good performance. By participating in both the ceremony and the dance, I showed dedication, teamwork, and school spirit, proving that I did not just attend the event but truly contributed to its success.

4. If I were to teach this topic/subject to a classmate, how would I explain it?

Activities like pageants, crown passings, and group dances are not only for entertainment but are opportunities to show these values in action—for example, humility in passing a title and teamwork in performing together. I would explain that Values Month helps students understand that character is just as important as talent or academic success.

5. Why is it important to have an event (per subject)? Explain your answer.
It is important to have events like Values Month because they make learning more meaningful and memorable. Events like this also build confidence, leadership skills, unity, and cooperation among students while allowing us to express our talents. Most importantly, Values Month reminds us that the values we learn in school shape our attitudes and decisions in everyday life, preparing us not only for academic success but also for becoming responsible and respectful individuals in the future.
